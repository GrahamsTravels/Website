The Ten Best Movies of 2025

10. Eephus (Carson Lund)

The last days of an amateur baseball league, one final game before their home field is torn down forever as it stands in the way of progress. The cast of misfits that look to baseball as a grounding element in their lives. They might complain and bicker, but they all value this one hobby that lets them check out from the world around them. A one true constant that enables social connection for even those that cannot find the words to form connections themselves. They refuse to stop playing, even as the umpire has to leave, even as the daylight fades, because to leave is to let go of this time in their lives.

Among all American sports, baseball is the one that still seems like it is from another time. Baseball and football are modern, their current forms looking nothing like the past. But Baseball is something unique, sure the players are bigger, they hit harder and throw faster, but the weird and archaic rules still keep it looking like an artifact more than a corporate product. It’s still better consumed watching in person with a hot dog in one hand and a beer in the other than on a 50+ inch TV.

Eephus is about baseball, how it’s remained while the world around it changes, and by remaining so constant it’s a stand in for what we have lost, for anything we feel nostalgic about. The once and forever American pastime is a nostalgia machine, a portal to our past, something we cling to when the rest of the world is slippery.

9. Eddington (Ari Aster)

And here we have the anti-nostalgia movie, recalling the time we all collectively lost our minds just a short while ago. It’s a mirror, but an exaggerated one, taking our worst fears and paranoias about those around us and exploding them onto the screen. At Eddington’s core it’s about that most troubling trend in American society: our vanishing ability to communicate. Conversations about real issues, be they pandemics, sexual abuse or policing, devolve into cultural war flashpoints without a moment of real consideration. The film starts with an inability to communicate, and ends with violence.

Eddington is dense with ideas, almost to its detriment, jumping from one to another haphazardly. Aspiring student activists with dubious intentions turned right-wing grifters. The annoyance of the left, the danger of the right. Ignoring good old fashion corruption from moderate politicians to instead wildly toss around accusations of heinous crimes. The all-consuming power of money and business interests. We all bicker; they win. It’s too provoking, and too aligned with my personal views, for me to ignore, but I can’t blame those that find this movie version of a nihilistic rant beyond the point of entertainment or even comprehension.

8. No Other Choice (Park Chan-Wook)

The concept is about as good as they come: man gets fired, man wants new job, man kills to get it. Only can Koreans make late-stage capitalism this violent, or this fun. There is plenty of thematic depth to be had, but Chan-Wook is really here for a good time. Chan-Wook’s direction is flashy, showing off his vast filmmaking skill. Layered, complex shots that pull the eye in different directions. Shots at times that are more bravos and innovative than they actually contribute to the story. In one composite shot, a husband tires away digging a grave, while each shovel of dirt stirs his wife’s sleeping head. Another shot displays the artistry seen from the inside of a beer glass mid bomb shot.

The title becomes the film’s mantra, the simple and severe way that capitalism forces us into actions against our own morals. The way that capitalism makes devils out of all of us. Or even farther, it’s “we have no other choice” that becomes the excuse which corporations tell themselves to forgo any ethical constraints. Disposing of the need for a moral compass, all decisions can be outsourced to spreadsheets, competitiveness and the eternal drive to create more shareholder value.

7. Bugonia (Yorgos Lathiamos)

The movie that dares to ask “what if all CEOs are actually aliens.” A conspiracy theorist and his cousin kidnap a local business executive believing them to be an alien. Right up there with No Other Choice for best logline of the year. Like many stories in recent years it centers on the lost status of white men and how that has corrupted their fragile egos, leading them down a road towards violent and disastrous results. The true genius of the film is that it doesn’t just direct its energies towards judging the “crazy man.” Instead, it allows us to sit within his shoes, wonder ourselves if he is in fact… right. We have all seen society degrade around us, we may not agree with conspiracies but we understand their appeal in an increasingly ludicrous world.

Emma Stone makes the film work as she masterfully handles executive-speak, a language that truly does sound like an alien trying to sound human. All the while, she can bring just enough actual humanity to balance our expectations, continually wondering whether the conspiracy theories are mere theories or truth.

6. Sinners (Ryan Coogler)

There’s a scene in Sinners where Delta Slim (Delory Lindo) tells of his friend’s tragic death at the hands of racist violence before breaking into singing the blues. For the first time, it gave me an understanding of the origin of the blues: a musical genre born of being powerless in the face of violence and oppression. Being left with no avenues to deal with grief and anger beyond expressing it through song.

Sinners is ultimately a film about music, music as an expression of culture, music as resistance, music as assimilation. All the vampire shit is just window dressing. Music is perhaps the most universal element of human culture, it is what binds us together. And Sinners is as fine a film made about music as there ever was. There are two scenes that will always live on in my head. One is the time-bending exploration of musical history from African origins through the blues, rock, jazz all the way to hip hop. It’s a poetic compression of human history looking backwards and forwards through time, the history of culture bending in on itself. The second is the rendition of “Rocky Road to Dublin” by the vampire horde. It’s pure vibes, chanting and dancing around the devil’s fire in all its Imax glory.

That’s all I’ve really got to say about it. It’s a beautiful looking film that has a great script and a wide cast of excellent performances. It's a perfect genre movie, but it’s those musical scenes that elevate Sinners to something more.

5. Sentimental Value (Joachim Trier)

The world of character dramas has increasingly become one of the small screen in the prestige TV era. The long-form medium allows for deeper character exploration, and more time for arcs to play out. Leave the big screen for the big, the flashy, and let the deep, the meaningful play out on the small screen. But Sentimental Value doesn’t need 10 hours for rich and dynamic characters. Excellent writing paired with perfect acting allows for an economic delivery of character development. We truly understand the characters not because of what is said, but because of how they behave, and through the language of faces and bodies./p>

It’s a film that rewards deep watching, the anti-Netflix, nothing is stated out loud. You have to be paying attention to understand the nuances of each character, but understand you will. My favorite performance of the year is Stelland Skarsgard as Gustav Borg, an artist who can only communicate through his art. He so desperately wants to connect with his daughters but can never find the right words to do so. Equally good are Renate Reinsve and Inga Ibsdotter Lilleaas as said daughters, who each struggle to find their footing in a broken family dynamic. Or Elle Fanning as a Hollywood star slotted for Borg’s next film. Fanning pulls off the difficult trick of being a great actor pretending to be a poor one.

It’s a story about holding emotions in so that they bubble under the surface, rare ripples across the face the only hints of these inner feelings. What could be a better showcase for acting? And what could be more Scandinavian?

4. Sirat (Oliver Laxe)

Sometimes the vibes are off. You’re at a super cool and exclusive rave secluded in the mountains of Morrocco, but there’s a middle-aged dad and his son looking for their daughter / sister there, and you feel bad but it’s kind of weird. When the Moroccan military shuts the rave down, you can’t let that end the party, I mean you’re not really a tourist, you’re too cool for that. You keep going deeper into the desert looking for the next party, the father and son are following you, and it’s awkward, but they’re cute, and act as good mascots. You don’t really notice that half your friends are missing limbs, and maybe going from rave to rave in the Moroccan desert isn’t really the scene in your 40s.

Then the vibes go real bad. The synth score and beautifully shot vistas don’t sit as well. The sunset doesn’t look as pretty when things are going bad bad. And you have to be left wondering, how did I get here? Is chasing a vibe all there is to life?

3. It Was Just An Accident (Jafar Panahi)

Jafar Panahi is as flexible a filmmaker as they come, his films are so dedicated to neorealism that they walk a delicate line between truth and fiction, playing with the meta if not outright becoming a documentary. Panahi isn’t alone in his exploration of the line between truth and fiction in the Iranian film scene, acclaimed predecessor and mentor Abbas Kiorstami was well-known for this as well. It Was Just An Accident doesn’t dabble with a metadocumentarian style itself, but the improvisational filmmaking that makes Panahi great was certainly required making a film in secret. Panahi himself is banned from making films in his home country.

And this ban is the result of his frequent criticism aimed at the Iranian authoritarian regime. His newest film may not be a documentary, but it is rooted in his own personal experience as much as the films he starred in playing himself. The paranoia and confusion of not knowing who your oppressors are or what they look like comes from Panahi’s personal experiences being blindfolded in prison. The varied approaches to resistance and survival under an authoritarian regime that are debated on screen mirror debates Panahi heard amongst his fellow political prisoners while incarcerated.

It Was Just Accident is both one of the funniest and most terrifying films of the year. It’s hard to describe much about the plot of the film without spoiling it because the confusion of the plot is part of the greatness. The audience is more confused than the characters, but only just so. And this confusion is what can lead to great humor, but great fear as well.

2. The Secret Agent (Kleber Mendonca Filho)

It’s a hangout movie, it’s just that the folks you’re hanging out with range from refugees hiding in apartment blocks to the brutal enforcers of a dictatorial regime (an unfortunately good hang in their own right). The Secret Agent wants to take you to a place and time where society hangs under a veil of mistrust and fear. Sharks are found with human legs that may or may not belong to student protestors. Bodies linger under cardboard boxes for days on end at rural gas stations. Fascist cops try to make friends with a German immigrant, assuming he is a Nazi, when in fact he is a holocaust survivor.

When authoritarianism reigns, it’s power rather than truth and law that is tantamount. And surviving under such a regime is a saga all its own. The title is a misnomer, a bit of a joke. This isn’t a story of rebelling against authoritarianism, if it's a story of resistance that’s only because mere survival is resistance by itself. The film doesn’t shy away from the brutality of the times, but doesn’t wallow in it either. It’s a hangout comedy rife with adventure, as much as it is a tragedy. Because life must go on, one must learn to exist even when the world seems turned upside down to the point that fairness and justice are mere words. Carnival still rages, and both the fascists and those they hunt enjoy the festivities.

The storytelling is fragmented, flowing unevenly from one scene to the next, plot threads are picked up and dropped, only to be found again somewhere else in the script. At first glance it seems like faulty editing, but this structure mimics the historical record of the time it is depicting. Fascist Brazil where records are sparse, people can be disappeared without a trace, even without any proof they ever existed at all. How can one know what happened when the media at the time was more focused on hyperbolic tales of folklore than any true events (a fact that is baked into the text of the movie). The film weaves webs that are as difficult to unravel for the viewer as it would be for an historian to know what truly did happen on the streets of Recife all those years ago.

1. One Battle After Another (Paul Thomas Anderson)

When a great film can mirror the social anxieties of the day it can elevate that film. We will always be interested in themes that touch on the topics we think about beyond the screen. Case in point, in recent years some of the best films have highlighted rising income inequality and last-stage capitalistic decline.This year, five of my ten favorite films are paranoid thrillers (the top 3 plus Bugonia and Eddington). And the top three are all films about how to survive under authoritarianism.

So, clearly you can see what is on my mind in an era where truth is hard to unravel from conspiracy and individual liberty appears to be on the wane. Maybe One Battle After Another is my favorite because it is the most relatable of these films to me as an American. It is a film so rooted in our current political moment that it feels impossible this film was fully written and filmed before the last election was even decided. There’s certainly an element to our preferences being directed by what connects with us in a moment in time.

But OBAA is a great film beyond that. The writing is fantastic with characters that are deeply complex. And those characters are brilliantly portrayed by every member of the wide cast (if they need to find a first-time winner of the Best Casting Oscar here is one as good as any). The filmmaking is dynamic, at times poetic as it weaves through scenes with a delicate touch. At other times, it is propulsive in thrilling action set-pieces. It’s a film that entertains first, then engages intellectually, then connects emotionally.




Honorable Mentions: Black Bag, Hamnet, Pillion, Marty Supreme, Weapons, If I Had Legs I'd Kick You, Tatami, 28 Years Later
