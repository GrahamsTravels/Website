Favorite Musuems

Honorable Mentions

Ho Chi Minh City Museum of Fine Arts - Ho Chi Minh City, Vietnam

Great collection of art about the preservation of traditional, independent culture, and the sacrifices made to do so.

National Museum of Singapore - Singapore

Very informative and direct museum that tells you the (government approved) history of Singapore.

National Gallery of Japan - Tokyo, Japan

Comprehensive survey of Japanese art and history, but only showcases a limited selection of their collection at any one time. Japanese museums often seem to display art that is relevant to the moment of the year, winter scenes in winter, art depicting cherry blossoms during the blossoming, etc. Beauty in that which is temporary.

Leeum Museum of Art - Seoul, South Korea

Beautiful collections in an interestingly-designed building

teamLab - Tokyo, Japan

A creative (and partially interactive) use of technology and light to make unique experiences.


Top 5 Favorite Musuems

5) South Australian Museum - Adelaide, Australia

Has a great collection of aboriginal art and artifacts, and some very interesting natural history sections that hit the right spot after I spent a few days in the outback.

4) Art Gallery of New South Wales - Sydney, Australia

Well-curated as the main collection takes you through major themes in Australian art, from early colonial art that (wrongly) depicted the country as untouched Eden, to more modern grappling with urbanization and environmental decay.

3) National Museum of Korea - Seoul, South Korea

A massive collection that showcases a comprehensive spread of Korean art, along with other Asian art. Perhaps too comprehensive, but everything you’d want to see is there.

2) Nagasaki Atomic Bomb Museum - Nagasaki, Japan

The top two museums for me were less a fun experience, but more a moving one. It’s hard to not be overcome with emotions when staring tragedies in the face.

1) War Remnants Museum - Ho Chi Minh City, Vietnam

A very simple museum, each room is primarily made up of still photographs on the walls. There are some referenced descriptions below if you want more context, but the additional context isn’t really needed. The power of the images speak for themselves.
